There is a story I used to tell myself about what a yoga teacher should look like.

Long hair. Flowing clothes. Serene expression. A certain kind of effortless beauty that looked, honestly, nothing like me.

I shaved my head for the first time six years ago, after a season of loss so heavy I stopped being able to perform being okay. Something had to go. I chose my hair.

What happened next surprised me.

I felt — for the first time in years — like myself.

Not the self I'd been performing. The actual one. The one who sweats during Savasana and cries in Pigeon Pose and sometimes laughs at completely inappropriate moments in class. That one.

Yoga teaches us that attachment is the root of suffering. We study this in sutras and workshops, nodding along. But sometimes the body has to learn it directly.

Letting go of my hair was letting go of the idea that I had to look a certain way to be worthy of being here. To be a teacher. To be loved.

Your mat doesn't care what you look like. Your breath doesn't care. The parts of you that yoga is actually trying to reach — the nervous system, the fascia, the wordless knowing in your chest — they are unbothered by your hair.

If there is something you are holding onto because you think it makes you acceptable, I want to gently ask: what would it feel like to put it down?

Not forever. Just for a breath.

See what's still there when you do.

*Veronica*
